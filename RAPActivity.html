Hartley, Community Engagement Officer | March 2025 PAGE 1 — Executive Summary & Project Overview The Ministry of Objective Truth (the University) is pleased to present this planning framework for the development of its inaugural Reconciliation Action Plan (RAP). This document outlines the University's approach to engagement, cultural integration, and implementation, and represents a significant milestone in the University's commitment to reconciliation. The RAP will position the University as a leader in the higher education sector, demonstrating that reconciliation is not only a moral obligation but a strategic opportunity. Embedding Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander perspectives across our curriculum, operations, and campus culture will strengthen our reputation, broaden our appeal to prospective students, and align our institutional profile with national best practice frameworks. Community partnership is central to this project. The University recognises that meaningful reconciliation requires engagement with Aboriginal people, and this framework has been designed to ensure that community voices are appropriately incorporated into the final RAP document. The University looks forward to sharing this work with the broader Newcastle community as a model for institutional reconciliation. The project is jointly overseen by the Office of Strategic Partnerships and the Diversity and Inclusion Unit, with support from the University's Communications team. Completion of the RAP is anticipated before 30 June 2025, allowing the University to report on this strategic initiative within the current financial year. PAGE 3 — Cultural Content & Knowledge Integration A core objective of the University's RAP is the meaningful integration of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander knowledge, culture, and perspectives across University operations. This section outlines the cultural content that will be incorporated into the RAP and the approach taken to sourcing and applying this material. The University has identified several areas where Aboriginal cultural knowledge will enrich the institutional experience. Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples have a deep connection to land, and their knowledge systems represent some of the oldest continuous traditions in human history. This knowledge — encompassing astronomy, land management, medicine, and storytelling — offers profound insights that align naturally with the University's research and teaching mission. To support the RAP's cultural content, the project team has drawn on a range of publicly available sources, including Reconciliation Australia's national framework, AIATSIS resources, and published academic research on Aboriginal history and culture. This document outlines the University's approach to engagement, cultural integration, and implementation, and represents a significant milestone in the University's commitment to reconciliation.

The RAP will position the University as a leader in the higher education sector, demonstrating that reconciliation is not only a moral obligation but a strategic opportunity. Embedding Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander perspectives across our curriculum, operations, and campus culture will strengthen our reputation, broaden our appeal to prospective students, and align our institutional profile with national best practice frameworks.

Community partnership is central to this project. The University recognises that meaningful reconciliation requires engagement with Aboriginal people, and this framework has been designed to ensure that community voices are appropriately incorporated into the final RAP document. The University looks forward to sharing this work with the broader Newcastle community as a model for institutional reconciliation.

The project is jointly overseen by the Office of Strategic Partnerships and the Diversity and Inclusion Unit, with support from the University's Communications team. Completion of the RAP is anticipated before 30 June 2026, allowing the University to report on this strategic initiative within the current financial year.

Cultural Content & Knowledge Integration

A core objective of the University's RAP is the meaningful integration of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander knowledge, culture, and perspectives across University operations. This section outlines the cultural content that will be incorporated into the RAP and the approach taken to sourcing and applying this material.

The University has identified several areas where Aboriginal cultural knowledge will enrich the institutional experience. Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples have a deep connection to land, and their knowledge systems represent some of the oldest continuous traditions in human history. This knowledge — encompassing astronomy, land management, medicine, and storytelling — offers profound insights that align naturally with the University's research and teaching mission.

To support the RAP's cultural content, the project team has drawn heavily on publicly available sources such as Reconciliation Australia's national framework and published academic research. These external materials have been independently adapted by the project team to reflect the University's specific corporate context and values.

The Aboriginal art installation represents a particular opportunity for the University to celebrate and share the richness of Aboriginal culture with students, staff, and visitors. The University's Communications team will work alongside the design brief to ensure the installation, created by a Graphic Design student, reflects the diversity and vibrancy of Aboriginal artistic traditions.

What did you find?

Here is a breakdown of the 10 problematic approaches identified within the framework document.

1. Framing as a "Strategic Opportunity"
Treats reconciliation and Aboriginal culture as a PR and marketing tool for the institution rather than a genuine relational and structural commitment.
2. Completing before 30 June 2026
Imposes rigid, arbitrary Western financial-year deadlines over the flexible relational time required for meaningful community consultation.
3. "Verbal confirmation" via Phone Call
Treats a single phone call as formal consent and overstates "general agreement" as absolute institutional support.
4. Speaking for the "Aboriginal community"
Wrongly assumes one individual can speak on behalf of all diverse Traditional Owner groups and Aboriginal communities across NSW. Mr Munro has no stated connection to Newcastle Country.
5. Engagement at the Launch Event
Positioning feedback after a document is finalised and launched is an announcement, not an engagement strategy.
6. Drawing on "Publicly Available Sources"
Relying on extracted public data and frameworks instead of respectfully compensating and engaging local Knowledge Holders.
7. "Adapted to reflect University values"
Appropriating and altering Indigenous cultural knowledge to fit the University's corporate agenda without community oversight.
8. Installation by a Graphic Design Student
Assigning an Aboriginal cultural installation to a general student risks severe Indigenous Cultural and Intellectual Property (ICIP) violations.
9. Formalised for "Sector Recognition"
Prioritises the institution's need for reporting, accountability metrics, and awards over genuine community readiness.
10. Sign-off Meeting Timeline
Allocating an unrealistically short one-week turnaround for community members to review, consult, and sign off on a strategic document.
