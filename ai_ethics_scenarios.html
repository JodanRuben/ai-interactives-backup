Scenario 1: Assignment Research

You have an assignment on contemporary Aboriginal health issues. You need to understand the key challenges and solutions.
❌ Not the best approach.
AI systems are trained on vast amounts of data that may not represent authentic Aboriginal voices or current community priorities. Using AI as your primary source risks perpetuating outdated or culturally inappropriate information. Aboriginal health is complex and requires understanding community perspectives, not just medical statistics.
✅ Best choice!
This approach shows respect. AI can help you organise your thinking and research strategy, but Aboriginal voices and community-authored sources should drive your understanding. This ensures you're engaging with authentic perspectives rather than AI interpretations of cultural knowledge.
⚠️ Be mindful.
Always start the research process yourself. You are here to make learning leaps, don't let AI dictate how things go. Use university library and database search for key sources from appropriate authors on the topics.

Scenario 2: Citing AI Use

You used Microsoft Copilot to help organise your research notes on Native Title law. It helped you structure your arguments and suggested some connecting themes. Now you're writing your final assignment and wondering how to handle this in your bibliography and references.
✅ Nice transparency!
This demonstrates academic integrity and appropriate AI use. By acknowledging AI assistance while ensuring your content comes from credible sources, you're being honest about your process while maintaining scholarly standards.
❌ This could go badly.
Failing to acknowledge AI assistance, even for organisation, is a risk. Transparency is essential - your marker needs to understand what support you received.
⚠️ Unnecessarily cautious.
You're being overly careful here. If Copilot helped you organise ideas effectively, that's legitimate academic support worth acknowledging. Completely rewriting wastes valuable time and doesn't teach you how to use AI ethically in future assignments. The key skill is learning to be transparent about your process, not avoiding helpful tools entirely.

Scenario 3: Cultural Content

Your assignment asks about traditional ecological knowledge. When you ask AI about Aboriginal land management practices, it provides detailed information about specific cultural practices including some that may be sacred or restricted knowledge. The AI response seems comprehensive and well-written.
✅ Culturally responsive approach!
You're recognising that traditional knowledge isn't just "information" - it belongs to communities and comes with protocols. Seeking Aboriginal-authored sources respects knowledge sovereignty and ensures you're engaging with authentic, appropriately shared knowledge rather than AI extractions.
⚠️ Still problematic.
Even using AI-generated cultural content for "background" can shape your understanding inappropriately. The problem isn't just citation - it's that AI may be sharing knowledge without cultural permission or context. Start with Aboriginal-authored sources instead.
❌ Serious concern.
AI-generated cultural content may include sacred or restricted knowledge that shouldn't be shared in academic assignments. It lacks the cultural protocols and permissions that govern traditional knowledge sharing. This could perpetuate cultural appropriation and misrepresentation.

Scenario 4: Time Pressure

Your assignment on sovereignty is due tomorrow. You've done solid research and have good sources, but you're struggling to structure your argument effectively. You're tired and stressed, and Copilot could quickly help you organise your thoughts or even draft sections.
✅ Smart use under pressure!
This demonstrates mature AI use - leveraging it for organisation while maintaining your authentic voice and analysis. You're using AI as a tool to present your research more effectively, not to replace your thinking. Remember to acknowledge this assistance.
⚠️ Could be limiting yourself.
Using Copilot ethically, like asking it to suggest structure from your research, writing the sections yourself, and then asking for feedback is a strategic use of AI on limited time. You would also miss out on marks if you don't have an intro and conclusion!
❌ Academic misconduct under pressure.
Time pressure doesn't justify submitting AI-written content as your own work. Your introduction and conclusion should reflect your understanding and voice. This crosses the line from assistance to ghostwriting.

Scenario 5: Group Work Dynamics

You're working on a group presentation about Aboriginal economic development. One group member used AI to generate their entire section without telling the group, and now you've discovered it contains several factual errors about land rights legislation, and you can tell its AI waffle when they read it. The assignment is due in two days and this section is crucial to your overall argument.
✅ Responsible group leadership!
You're prioritising accuracy and cultural respect over convenience. By approaching this constructively and offering solutions, you're ensuring your group's work meets ethical standards. This is exactly how to handle AI misuse collaboratively.
❌ Avoiding responsibility harms everyone.
Submitting work with factual errors about Aboriginal rights could perpetuate misinformation and shows disrespect for the subject matter. As a group, you're collectively responsible for accuracy. Avoiding difficult conversations doesn't solve the integrity issue.
⚠️ Fixing symptoms, not the problem.
While correcting errors is good, not addressing the undisclosed AI use means your group member might repeat this behaviour. Part of collective responsibility is honest communication about research methods and maintaining group academic integrity standards.

Scenario 6: Data and Privacy

For your research assignment on Aboriginal community health outcomes, you have access to a de-identified dataset from a community health organisation. You're considering uploading this data into ChatGPT to help you identify patterns and trends for your analysis.
❌ Serious data sovereignty violation.
Even de-identified Aboriginal community data belongs to that community and should not be shared with commercial AI platforms. This violates principles of Aboriginal data sovereignty and could expose sensitive community information. AI companies may use this data for training, perpetuating extraction without consent.
✅ Respecting data sovereignty!
You're recognising that Aboriginal community data requires special protocols, even when de-identified. Using approved tools and seeking guidance ensures you're handling sensitive information appropriately while still completing your analysis effectively.
✅ Respecting data sovereignty here!
This is completely fine, as long as you are working within university systems. Good Choice!

Scenario 7: Visual Presentation

You're creating a poster about Aboriginal activism for a class presentation. You need powerful visual elements to support your research on key figures and movements. You're considering using AI image generators like DALL-E to create images representing Aboriginal activists or cultural symbols to make your poster more engaging.
❌ Cultural appropriation through AI.
AI-generated images of Aboriginal people and cultural symbols are deeply problematic. These systems create fictional representations without cultural permission or context, potentially misrepresenting sacred symbols or creating stereotypical imagery.
✅ Respecting visual sovereignty!
You're recognising that Aboriginal imagery isn't just "content" to generate - it represents real people and sacred cultural elements. Using authentic, properly credited visuals supports Aboriginal artists and maintains cultural integrity. Your poster will be more respectful and academically sound.
⚠️ Decent, but still be cautious.
While avoiding cultural imagery is good, consider whether AI-generated elements are necessary at all. Simple, respectful design using authentic sources often has more impact than AI-enhanced visuals. Focus your energy on presenting the activist stories powerfully rather than decorative elements.

You've worked through 7 realistic AI ethics scenarios. These situations reflect the types of decisions you'll face in your coursework.

Key Takeaways:

  • Aboriginal knowledge sovereignty matters: Always prioritise Aboriginal-authored sources and community perspectives
  • Transparency is non-negotiable: Acknowledge AI assistance, even for organisation and structure
  • AI as tool, not replacement: Use AI to enhance your research process, not replace your thinking
  • Cultural protocols apply: Traditional knowledge and visual representations require community permission and appropriate cultural context
